## Introduction

The sperm whale is the **largest toothed animal on Earth** and one of the most instantly recognizable marine mammals. Its most defining feature is the head: enormous, box-shaped, and squared-off at the front, accounting for nearly **40% of total body length**. The body is slightly laterally compressed, with dark grey or grey-brown skin and a paler underside. The lower jaw is narrow and small relative to the massive head, and **teeth are found only on the lower jaw**.

Sperm whales are highly social animals with tight-knit pod bonds. They are masters of echolocation, using a distinctive form of sound known as **burst-pulse clicks** — very loud, low-frequency pulses — to navigate and to stun or incapacitate prey. They are among the deepest-diving mammals on Earth, capable of descending over 1,000 metres in pursuit of deep-sea squid.

## Threats

**Mass stranding**
Because sperm whales are strongly social, one of their most significant risks is **mass stranding**. When a pod leader navigates incorrectly, when the group chases prey too far into shallow water, or when one member becomes sick or stranded, the rest of the pod will often follow — sometimes resulting in the entire group stranding together.

**Marine plastic pollution**
A documented case of a **dwarf sperm whale** (*Kogia sima*, a smaller relative) stranding dead in Thailand found its stomach packed with plastic bags. The accumulated plastic blocked the digestive tract, caused illness, and ultimately killed the animal. This case illustrates how plastic pollution poses a concrete, fatal threat to sperm whales as well as to other cetaceans.

**Other threats**
Like all large whales, sperm whales are also affected by:
- Entanglement in commercial fishing gear (bycatch)
- Acoustic disturbance from seismic surveys and military sonar, which disrupts their sophisticated echolocation-based communication and navigation
- Vessel strikes

## Conservation solutions

**Legal protection**
Sperm whales and all whale species in Thai waters are protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535 and its 2019 amendment. There is an absolute prohibition on hunting, trading, and possessing the animals.

**Mass stranding response**
Responding to a mass stranding of sperm whales requires rapid coordination of large numbers of people and clear task allocation. The protocol:

1. **Triage and first aid** — prioritize live animals. Support each animal in the water if possible, which reduces stress and prevents organ compression from the animal's own body weight.
2. **Vital care on land** — if animals are on the beach, keep water out of the blowhole at all times and never cover the blowhole with any material. Protect the eyes from sand and direct sunlight. Pour water over the skin continuously to maintain moisture and reduce heat.
3. **Return to sea as a group** — if animals are assessed as strong enough to swim and breathe independently, guide and encourage the entire pod back into deep water together. Mother-calf pairs must be kept together; separating them often causes the adults to return to shore.
4. **Post-mortem for fatalities** — stranded animals that die undergo necropsy to identify the cause of death (plastic ingestion, injury, disease), generating data for future prevention planning.
