## Introduction

The blue whale is the **largest animal known to have ever existed on Earth**.

Its body is long and streamlined. The head is wide and distinctly **U-shaped** when viewed from above — a key identification feature — and carries a single ridge running along the top. The coloring is primarily blue-gray on the back and slightly paler on the underside. The back and flanks are patterned with **irregular blue or light-gray mottled spots** that are distributed across the body. This mottled pattern varies between individuals and can potentially be used for identification.

## Threats

Although no specific stranding incidents in Thai waters are documented in the source material, blue whales face the same suite of threats as other large baleen whales:

**Bycatch**
Blue whales can become accidentally entangled in commercial fishing gear in areas where their feeding range overlaps with fisheries activity.

**Marine pollution**
- **Plastic ingestion** — floating plastic debris enters their filter-feeding system.
- **Chemical contamination** — industrial pollutants and agricultural runoff accumulate through the food chain.
- **Acoustic pollution** — seismic surveys for oil and gas exploration and naval sonar disrupt the blue whale's low-frequency communication and long-range navigation calls. This can cause disorientation and stranding.

**Vessel strikes**
Blue whales surface slowly and are at significant risk of being struck by large ships in busy shipping lanes.

**Climate and environmental change**
Rapid changes in sea conditions — storms, sudden tidal shifts, unusual ocean temperatures — can affect prey distribution and navigation, contributing to stranding risk.

## Conservation solutions

**Designation as Reserved Wildlife**
The 12 May 2022 resolution to list the blue whale as Reserved Wildlife under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act brings the strictest penalties in Thai law to bear on any hunting, trading, possession, or exploitation of the species.

**Surveys and database building**
DMCR actively monitors sighting reports from social media and the public volunteer network to build a database of blue whale distribution, habitat use, and migration routes in Thai waters. This data will underpin future conservation measures.

**Stranding preparedness**
A standardized response manual for rare marine animal strandings has been developed and distributed. This manual provides the operational framework for assessing the animal, administering first aid, and increasing the animal's chances of survival when a blue whale is found injured or stranded.

**Long-term outlook**
Because blue whale sightings in Thai waters remain rare, the current focus is on population surveillance and building the legal and institutional infrastructure to protect the species as encounters become better documented.
