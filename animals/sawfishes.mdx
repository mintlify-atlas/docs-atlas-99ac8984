# Sawfish

**Thai name:** ปลาฉนากยักษ์  
**Scientific name:** *Pristis pristis* (largetooth sawfish)  
**Codebase ID:** `sawfishes`  
**Polygon shards:** 196  
**Deep link:** `/detail?id=sawfishes`

<Warning>
  Sawfish are classified as **Critically Endangered** on the IUCN Red List and listed on **CITES Appendix I**, prohibiting all international commercial trade. In Thailand, sawfish are **Regionally Extinct** (RE) in the wild — there have been no confirmed sightings of live individuals in nature for over **30 years**. Only rostrum teeth (the saw) are occasionally found in shrines and antique shops. Sawfish are protected under Thailand's Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (2019).
</Warning>

## Introduction

The giant sawfish is one of the most striking and unusual fish on the planet. A fully grown adult can reach **7 metres in length**. Its most recognizable feature is the **rostrum** — a long, flat snout lined with **16–20 pairs of sharp, thin teeth** arranged like the blade of a saw. The upper body and flanks are yellowish-grey; the belly is creamy white.

Sawfish are distributed across tropical coastal waters. They are demersal animals — bottom-dwellers — found in shallow coral reef habitats no deeper than 10 metres, estuaries, and even freshwater river systems. **Juveniles spend their first 4–5 years in freshwater** before migrating downstream to the sea.

Sawfish grow slowly and reach sexual maturity late:
- Males: ready to breed at **2.4–3.0 m body length**
- Females: at **2.8–3.0 m**
- Gestation: approximately **5 months**, giving birth to **1–13 live pups**
- Lifespan: approximately **35 years**

This combination of slow growth, late maturity, and low birth rate makes sawfish populations exceptionally slow to recover from overfishing.

## Threats

**Fishing gear entanglement**
The toothed rostrum catches extremely easily in fishing nets — gill nets, trawls, and deep-water traps. Even though most entanglement is accidental (bycatch), it typically results in the sawfish being killed or brought aboard.

**Commercial trade**
Multiple parts of the sawfish have high market value:
- The **rostrum (saw)** is highly prized as decoration, a collectible, or a charm, fetching high prices.
- Fins are sold for shark fin soup.
- The flesh is consumed.
- Live sawfish are caught for display in aquariums due to their unusual appearance.

**Habitat degradation**
Coastal development, river pollution, and sedimentation destroy the estuaries and shallow coastal habitats that sawfish depend on throughout their life cycle.

## Conservation solutions

Even though sawfish are presumed regionally extinct in Thailand, protective measures remain in force and response infrastructure is being maintained against the possibility of future sightings.

**Legal protection**
- Sawfish are protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (2019).
- Listed on **CITES Appendix I** — international trade is absolutely prohibited.
- Import and export are also controlled under Thailand's Trade Controls Act B.E. 2522 (1979).

**Fishing gear restrictions**
Prohibitions on trawls and push nets (*oun roon*) in coastal zones reduce the risk of large bottom-dwelling fish being swept up accidentally, though coverage of all relevant habitats is not yet complete.

**National Plan of Action**
Thailand has developed a **National Plan of Action for the Conservation and Management of Sharks (NPOA-Sharks)**, which covers sawfish as elasmobranch species and provides a framework for long-term conservation planning.

**Reporting network and database**
A network for reporting rare marine animal sightings has been established so that, if a sawfish is ever encountered again in Thai waters, the sighting can be captured, verified, and responded to rapidly.

<Note>
  If you ever encounter what appears to be a sawfish rostrum (saw) in Thailand — in a temple, a market, or an antique shop — it is a protected wildlife product. You can report it to DMCR through the rare marine animal reporting network.
</Note>
