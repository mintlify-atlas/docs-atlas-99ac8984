## Introduction

The orca, also known as the killer whale, is the **apex predator of the marine ecosystem**. It is the most widely distributed marine mammal on Earth after humans.

Physically, the orca has a robust, powerfully built body with a rounded head and a short beak. Its most striking features are:
- **Large paddle-like pectoral fins**
- A towering dorsal fin — up to **2 metres tall in males**; shorter and curved in females
- **10–14 pairs of large, conical, slightly curved teeth** on both upper and lower jaws
- The classic black-and-white coloration, with a white eye patch and a grey saddle patch behind the dorsal fin

Orcas are highly social and live in stable family groups (pods). They are sophisticated hunters and communicate using complex, pod-specific vocal dialects.

**Role in the Low-Poly Ocean experience**
The orca holds a unique position in the experience as the **natural predator of several other animals in the list**, including leatherback turtles, dugongs, dolphins, and manta rays. When prey animals are chased by orcas, the pursuit can cause the prey to panic and race into shallow water, triggering decompression sickness or stranding. This predator-prey relationship is why the orca appears alongside the endangered species.

## Threats

In the context of this experience, the orca's primary role is as a natural threat to the other animals featured. The content focuses on how orca predation events can indirectly cause strandings:

- Prey animals (turtles, dugongs, dolphins, manta rays) are chased into shallow water, become disoriented or injured, and strand.
- The rapid ascent from depth during escape can cause decompression sickness in cetaceans.
- Bite wounds from an unsuccessful orca attack can result in infection or serious injury.

Orcas themselves face threats typical of large marine predators:
- **Bycatch** in fishing gear
- **Acoustic pollution** (sonar, seismic surveys) that disrupts communication and navigation
- **Mass stranding** — if the pod leader navigates incorrectly, the entire group may follow onto shore
- **Marine pollution** — orcas accumulate high levels of persistent organic pollutants (PCBs, DDT) in their blubber because of their position at the top of the food chain

## Conservation solutions

**Legal protection**
All whale and dolphin species found in Thai waters are protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535. This protection is the primary legal safeguard for any orca entering Thai territorial waters.

**Stranding response**
If an orca strands, the response follows the standard protocol for large cetaceans:
1. Support the animal in the water to maintain normal posture. Take extreme care to keep water out of the blowhole and never cover it.
2. If the animal is on land, dig depressions under the pectoral fins to relieve organ compression. Apply wet cloths to the body (leave the blowhole clear) and pour water continuously to keep the animal cool and moist.
3. For **mass strandings** — which can occur when a pod leader navigates incorrectly or chases prey into shallow water — rapid coordination of personnel and equipment is essential. The goal is to guide and encourage the entire pod back into deep water at once, keeping mother-calf pairs together to reduce the risk of them returning to shore.
