# Irrawaddy Dolphin

<Warning>
  The Irrawaddy dolphin is classified as **Endangered** on the IUCN Red List and listed on **CITES Appendix I** — indicating it is critically threatened with extinction globally. In Thailand it has been elevated to **Reserved Wildlife** (สัตว์ป่าสงวน) status under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act, the highest tier of legal protection.
</Warning>

## Introduction

The Irrawaddy dolphin stands out immediately from other dolphins: it has **no beak**. The head is round and bulbous, the dorsal fin is small with a blunt tip and sits toward the rear half of the body, and the back is blue-grey while the belly is noticeably paler.

Teeth count: **8–19 pairs on the upper jaw** and **11–18 pairs on the lower jaw**.

Irrawaddy dolphins are a nearshore resident species — they stay close to coastlines rather than venturing into open ocean. This makes them particularly vulnerable to all the human activities that take place along the coast.

## Threats

**Coastal infrastructure**
Bamboo wave-break structures and other coastal constructions installed in feeding areas can act as traps. During falling tides, dolphins may be unable to escape back to deeper water. A documented mass stranding of **five Irrawaddy dolphins** occurred at a bamboo wave-break in Samut Sakhon province, illustrating how directly this threat operates.

**Fishing gear**
Accidental bycatch in nets — and injuries or deaths from illegal fishing methods — are among the leading causes of harm. Bottom-feeding behavior makes these dolphins especially likely to encounter gear.

**Marine pollution**
Ingesting plastic waste causes intestinal blockages. Pollutants accumulate in tissues through the food chain. Noise pollution from boat traffic and sonar disrupts echolocation-based navigation and communication, causing disorientation and stranding.

**Natural causes**
Mass stranding can also arise when a pod leader navigates incorrectly while chasing prey into shallow water, causing the whole group to follow onto shore.

## Conservation solutions

**Legal protection**
The Irrawaddy dolphin is strictly protected under Thailand's Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act. Hunting, trading, or possessing the animal is prohibited.

**Stranding response protocol**
When a live dolphin is found stranded:
1. Support the animal in the water so it can maintain normal buoyancy. Keep water and sand out of the **blowhole** (located on top of the head). Never cover the blowhole with cloth.
2. If the dolphin is on land, dig depressions under each pectoral fin to reduce pressure on internal organs. Drape a wet cloth over the body (leaving the blowhole and dorsal fin exposed) and pour water regularly to maintain moisture and reduce body heat.
3. Use appropriate transport equipment — a canvas stretcher with carrying poles sized for the animal — to move it to a rehabilitation pool or to a safe release site without causing additional injuries.
4. Injured or ill animals go to a rehabilitation pool under close veterinary care until they are assessed as ready for release.

**Reducing upstream threats**
Long-term reduction of threats requires removing or modifying coastal structures in known dolphin feeding areas and enforcing bans on illegal fishing gear near the coast.
