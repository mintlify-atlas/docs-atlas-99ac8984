# Ronin / Bowmouth Guitarfish

**Thai name:** ปลาโรนิน  
**Scientific name:** *Rhina ancylostoma*

**Polygon shards:** 372

<Warning>
  The bowmouth guitarfish is classified as **Critically Endangered** on the IUCN Red List and listed on **CITES Appendix II**, which controls international trade. In Thailand it is a **Protected Wildlife** species under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (2019, announced 2018). Hunting, trading, or possessing the animal or its products without authorization is prohibited.
</Warning>

## Introduction

The bowmouth guitarfish — known colloquially as the *ronin* in this experience — is a member of the shark-ray order (Rhinopristiformes): a group that bridges the gap between sharks and rays. It is unmistakable in appearance:

- The head is **large and broad**, with a **semicircular (rounded) snout** — the rounded tip is the key feature that distinguishes the ronin from the closely related shovelnose ray (*ronin* vs. *ronan* in some Thai usage), which has a pointed snout.
- A **row of sharp bony ridges (thorns)** runs along the brow line above the eyes and down the center of the back. These ridges — called "ronin thorns" or "ronin heads" locally — are the feature that makes this fish both famous and tragically targeted.
- Adults reach **2.7–3 m in length**.

The bowmouth guitarfish is a **demersal species**: it lives and feeds on the seafloor, hunting crustaceans (shrimp, lobster, crab) in sandy or muddy habitats associated with coral reefs and coastlines, at depths from **3 m to 90 m**. It is found across the Indian and Pacific Oceans; in Thailand, it occurs on both the Gulf of Thailand side and the Andaman side, though sightings are infrequent.

Reproduction is viviparous (live-bearing with a yolk sac): females give birth to **2–11 pups** per litter, with newborns measuring approximately **46–48 cm** at birth.

## Threats

**Illegal trade — bony head ridges**
The most specific and acute threat to the bowmouth guitarfish is the illegal demand for the bony dorsal ridges on its head. These ridges are carved into **rings, bracelets, and lucky charms**, based on the belief that they protect against water-related harm and bring good fortune. The high black-market value of the ridges incentivizes direct targeted hunting of the species.

The ventral (belly) skin is also sold for making leather goods such as bags.

**Bycatch and commercial fishing**
Because the bowmouth guitarfish is a bottom-dweller, it is frequently caught accidentally in:
- Bottom trawls
- Purse seines
- Crab trap nets

Entanglement causes severe lacerations from net lines and often results in death or serious injury. Fins and flesh are also consumed.

**Aquarium capture**
The unusual appearance of the species makes live individuals desirable for commercial aquarium display, creating additional demand for wild capture.

**Habitat loss**
Coastal development destroys the sandy and muddy seafloor habitats where the bowmouth guitarfish feeds, directly reducing carrying capacity for the species.

## Conservation solutions

**Legal protection in Thailand**
The bowmouth guitarfish is protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (2019). Import, export, and possession of the animal or its products (including the bony ridges) without authorization is illegal. International trade is regulated under CITES Appendix II.

**Rescue and rehabilitation**
A documented rescue case from Phang Nga province illustrates the response when an animal is injured: a bowmouth guitarfish found entangled in a crab trap net with its tail nearly severed was brought to a marine research center, where veterinarians cleaned and treated the wound before transferring the animal to a recovery facility. A rescue reporting network enables similar interventions when incidents are reported.

**Fishing gear restrictions**
Prohibitions on trawls and push nets in coastal zones reduce — though do not eliminate — the risk of accidental capture. Further zoning and enforcement in known habitat areas would increase protection.

**National Plan of Action for Sharks**
Thailand's **NPOA-Sharks** provides a long-term framework for managing and conserving elasmobranch species, including the bowmouth guitarfish, through coordinated conservation planning.

<Tip>
  The bony ridge artifacts sold as "ronin head" charms or rings in Thailand are protected wildlife products. If you encounter them for sale, you can report it to the Department of National Parks, Wildlife and Plant Conservation (DNP) or DMCR.
</Tip>
