# Dugong

<Warning>

</Warning>

## Introduction

Dugongs are fully marine mammals that descended from a land-dwelling ancestor shared with elephants roughly **55 million years ago**. They returned to the sea and never came back to land.

Their body is torpedo-shaped — rounder and broader than a dolphin's — with no dorsal fin but a large fluke and peduncle. The thick skin is grey-pink or grey-brown, with a paler underside. Short sparse hairs cover most of the body, while the area around the mouth carries dense, coarse bristles. The eyes and ears are tiny; the ears are just open holes with no outer flap. Valve-covered nostrils allow the dugong to surface and breathe every **1–2 minutes**.

Adult dugongs are **2.5–3 m long** and weigh **250–420 kg**. Newborns measure 1–1.5 m and weigh 20–35 kg. Age can be estimated by counting growth layers in the tusks — males develop protruding tusks around age 13–14. Females have mammary glands under their pectoral fins.

Dugongs swim slowly, typically at **1.8–2.2 km/h**. Their dense, heavy skeleton helps them sink to the seafloor to graze. Their primary food is seagrass — species such as *Halophila ovalis*, *Cymodocea rotundata*, and *Halodule uninervis* — consuming **2–5% of body weight daily**. Although dugongs have no natural weapons, their large size, thick skin, and rapid blood clotting offer some protection. They can live up to **70 years**.

In Thailand, dugongs are found on both the Gulf of Thailand side (Chonburi, Rayong, Chanthaburi, Trat, Chumphon, Surat Thani, Pattani) and the Andaman side. The highest concentrations are around **Ko Libong and Ko Muk in Trang province**, which historically account for roughly 49% of all stranding records in Thailand.

## Threats

**Human threats**
- **Bycatch** — dugongs share feeding grounds with fishing operations and frequently become accidentally entangled in nets. Illegal fishing methods (explosives, electric fishing, poison) kill dugongs and other marine animals each year.
- **Coastal development** — bamboo wave-break structures and other intertidal constructions can trap dugongs in shallow water during falling tides, causing stranding.
- **Marine pollution** — ingestion of plastic waste and accumulation of pollutants through the food chain causes illness and death.
- **Noise pollution** — seismic surveys for petroleum and fishing boat sonar disrupt communication and navigation.
- **Poaching** — tusk tips have been found removed from stranded carcasses during post-mortems.
- **Boat strikes** — propeller injuries cause serious wounds and death.

**Natural threats**
- Natural predators such as sharks and orcas can cause dugongs to panic and race into shallow water, triggering decompression sickness or stranding.
- Storms, strong waves, and rapid tidal drops leave feeding dugongs stranded in shallows.
- Natural disease.

## Conservation solutions

<Note>
  Thailand's Department of Marine and Coastal Resources (DMCR) maintains an online reporting system at **mes.dmcr.go.th** where anyone can report dugong sightings and stranding events.
</Note>

**Emergency response (stranding or injury)**
When a live dugong is found stranded or injured:
1. Report the incident immediately through the conservation network so trained responders reach the site quickly.
2. Veterinarians and officers assess the animal. If healthy, it is released immediately. If injured (for example, entangled in gear), first aid is given and the dugong is transported to a recovery pool, then released once well.
3. If the dugong has died, a post-mortem is conducted to identify the exact cause of death (injury, ingested waste, natural disease) so that findings can inform future prevention.

**Legal protection**
The dugong is protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (2019). Hunting, trading, or possessing a dugong — including its parts — carries strict penalties. Certain harmful fishing gear types are prohibited in coastal zones.

**Research and monitoring**
DMCR conducts ongoing surveys of population numbers, distribution, and habitat use, and operates a national database for rare marine animal sightings (mes.dmcr.go.th). This data feeds into trend analysis and threat assessment.

**Community and tourism engagement**
Coastal communities, fishing groups, and tourism operators are recruited as partners to watch for and report threats to dugongs. Conservation campaigns promote reducing marine litter and encourage responsible wildlife tourism that does not disturb the animals.
