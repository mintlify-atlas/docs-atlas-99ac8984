<Warning>
  The giant manta ray is classified as **Endangered** on the IUCN Red List and listed on **CITES Appendix II**, which requires that international trade is controlled to avoid incompatibility with survival. In Thailand it is a **Protected Wildlife** species under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (2019, announced 2018).
</Warning>

## Introduction

The giant manta ray is the **largest ray species in the world**, with a disc width (wingspan) that can reach **7–9 metres**. Several features set it apart from other rays:

- The mouth is positioned at the **front tip of the body** (terminal mouth), unlike other rays whose mouths are on the underside.
- The top of the head has a **T-shaped black marking**.
- The underside has a gray-black border along the disc edge, with a scattered pattern of gray-black spots. These spot patterns are unique to each individual and can be used for **individual identification**.
- A small, vestigial spine ridge is present at the base of the tail behind the dorsal fin — a remnant of the stinging spine found in other rays.

Giant manta rays are filter feeders: they use specialized gill rakers to strain **plankton, small organisms, and fish eggs** from the water, swimming with their mouths open at the surface or at depth. They undertake long-distance migrations across oceans and regularly visit **cleaning stations** — underwater rocks or reef formations where cleaner fish remove parasites from the manta's body.

Despite living up to approximately **45 years**, manta rays reproduce very slowly. A female gives birth to just **one pup at a time**, with **4–5 years between births**. This low reproductive rate makes population recovery after hunting pressure extremely slow.

In Thailand, giant manta rays are rare on the Gulf of Thailand side but observed more regularly in the **Andaman Sea**. Key sites include Ko Bon, Ko Tachai, and Richelieu Rock in Phang Nga; Ko Racha Noi in Phuket; and Hin Daeng–Hin Muang in Krabi.

## Threats

**Fishing and bycatch**
Manta rays become accidentally entangled in commercial purse seines and trawl nets. Discarded fishing gear (ghost gear) wraps around their bodies and cephalic lobes (the horn-like head projections), cutting deeply and causing infection and death.

**International trade demand**
Gill rakers are in high demand for use in traditional Chinese medicine. Manta flesh is also consumed in some markets. This demand creates direct incentive to hunt the species and has driven rapid population declines globally.

**Irresponsible dive tourism**
Divers who chase, corner, or touch manta rays, or who use camera flash at close range, disrupt natural feeding and cleaning station visits. Even well-intentioned encounters, if too close or too frequent, stress the animals and reduce the value of cleaning station habitats.

## Conservation solutions

**Legal protection**
Giant manta rays are protected under Thailand's Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562. Import, export, and possession of the species — including its products — is prohibited without proper authorization.

**Research and citizen science**
DMCR collaborates with the global **Marine Megafauna Foundation (MMF)** and local dive networks to build a photo-ID database using the unique spot patterns on each manta's underside. This database tracks population size, movement patterns, and cross-border migration. Sightings reported by divers (citizen science) feed directly into the research.

**Rescue response**
When a manta ray is reported entangled in fishing gear, a team of trained divers and officers responds to cut away the nets or ropes. The animal is assessed for injury before release.

**Dive tourism guidelines**
Active campaigns promote responsible manta ray encounters:
- Maintain a respectful distance and do not chase or corner the animal.
- Do not touch the ray; physical contact damages the protective mucus layer on the skin.
- Avoid camera flash, which can startle the animal and interrupt feeding.

<Tip>
  Richelieu Rock in Phang Nga is one of the best sites in Southeast Asia to see giant manta rays. Choose dive operators who follow the Marine Megafauna Foundation's Code of Conduct for manta ray encounters.
</Tip>
