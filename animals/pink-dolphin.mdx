# Indo-Pacific Humpback Dolphin

**Thai name:** โลมาชมพู (Pink Dolphin)  
**Scientific name:** *Sousa chinensis*  
**Codebase ID:** `dolphin`

<Warning>
  The Indo-Pacific humpback dolphin is classified as **Vulnerable** on the IUCN Red List. In Thailand it is a **Protected Wildlife** species under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535 (1992), with strict prohibitions on hunting, trading, and captive breeding.
</Warning>

## Introduction

The Indo-Pacific humpback dolphin is widely known in Thailand as the "pink dolphin" — a name that describes the animal in its adult form, not its appearance at birth.

Physically, the dolphin has a round, melon-shaped head, a long and slender beak, and **28–38 pairs of teeth** on both upper and lower jaws. The most striking feature is how the body color changes across a lifetime:

- **Juvenile:** dark gray or dark blue-gray body
- **Sub-adult / young adult:** the gray lightens gradually and some individuals develop a gray-and-white mottled pattern
- **Mature adult:** the body transitions to **white or distinctly pink**, giving the species its common name

In Thailand, the primary habitat for this species is **Khanom-Don Sak Bay** in the Gulf of Thailand, where tour boats operate specifically to see them.

## Threats

**Irresponsible tourism**
Tour boats in Khanom-Don Sak Bay have been observed hand-feeding dolphins, which alters the animals' natural behavior and exposes them to contaminated food. This is one of the most immediate and localized threats the population faces.

**Fishing gear**
Accidental bycatch in fishing nets and injuries from illegal fishing methods are primary causes of injury and death. Boat traffic in feeding areas also causes propeller strikes.

**Marine pollution**
Ingested plastic waste causes intestinal blockages. Toxins accumulate in fatty tissue through the food chain.

**Natural causes**
Illness, age-related weakness, and fleeing natural predators such as sharks or orcas can all drive dolphins into shallow water and lead to stranding.

## Conservation solutions

**Legal protection**
The Indo-Pacific humpback dolphin, along with all dolphins and whales in Thai waters, is protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535. Hunting, trading, possessing, or breeding the species is strictly prohibited.

**Stranding response**
Standard rescue protocol when a live dolphin is found stranded:
1. Support the animal in the water to maintain normal posture. Do not let water enter the blowhole (on top of the head).
2. If the dolphin is on dry ground or a beach, dig depressions under the pectoral fins to relieve pressure on internal organs. Apply a wet cloth to the body (excluding the blowhole and dorsal fin) and pour water regularly to prevent overheating and skin drying.
3. Use a correctly sized canvas stretcher with carrying poles to move the animal. The stretcher must support the animal's full weight and prevent skin abrasion during transport.
4. Transport the dolphin to a rehabilitation pool large enough for it to swim, where a veterinarian can diagnose and treat injuries before release.

**Addressing root causes**
The most impactful long-term interventions are:
- Actively campaigning to **stop feeding dolphins in the wild**, particularly in Khanom-Don Sak Bay, to prevent behavioral changes and contamination.
- Raising public awareness about reducing marine litter, which is a leading cause of death for marine mammals through ingestion of floating plastic debris.

<Tip>
  If you visit Khanom-Don Sak Bay to watch the pink dolphins, keep a respectful distance, do not throw food into the water, and choose tour operators that follow wildlife-watching guidelines.
</Tip>
