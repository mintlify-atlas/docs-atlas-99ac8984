# Leatherback Turtle

<Warning>
  The leatherback turtle is classified as **Vulnerable** on the IUCN Red List and is a designated **Protected Wildlife** species under Thailand's Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act. It was elevated to **Reserved Wildlife** (สัตว์ป่าสงวน) status under the 2019 amendment, the highest level of legal protection in Thailand.
</Warning>

## Introduction

The leatherback turtle is the largest sea turtle in the world. A fully grown adult can reach a shell length of **210 cm** and weigh up to **900 kg**; most individuals that come ashore to nest are at least 150 cm long.

Unlike other sea turtles, the leatherback has no hard, scute-covered shell. Instead, its back is covered with thick, leathery black skin — often dotted with white patches — and seven longitudinal ridges run from head to tail. The head lacks scales, and the upper jaw has a characteristic three-notch bite. Hatchlings are black with white edges along the ridges; the white spots spread across the body as the turtle matures.

Leatherbacks spend their entire lives in the open ocean, coming ashore only during the nesting season. They feed primarily at the water's surface, where **jellyfish and sea sponges** make up the bulk of their diet. Sexual maturity is reached at around **13–14 years** of age. Females nest every 1–3 years, laying clutches between **December and February**. In Thailand, nesting beaches are found exclusively on the mainland coasts of **Phang Nga and Phuket** provinces.

## Threats

Threats to leatherback turtles fall into two categories: human activity and natural factors.

**Human threats**
- **Bycatch** — turtles become accidentally entangled in fishing nets, trawls, and longline hooks. Illegal fishing methods such as dynamite fishing and the use of nets in protected areas also cause casualties.
- **Plastic ingestion** — because leatherbacks feed on jellyfish, they frequently mistake floating plastic bags for food. Ingested plastic blocks the digestive tract, leading to illness and death.
- **Marine pollution** — chemical contamination and pollutants accumulate through the food chain.
- **Coastal development** — construction of seawalls and erosion barriers along the Phang Nga and Phuket nesting beaches blocks hatchlings from reaching the sea and can trap nesting females during falling tides.
- **Boat strikes** — propellers can inflict serious or fatal wounds.

**Natural threats**
- Severe weather, storm surges, and rapid tidal changes can strand turtles and damage nesting beaches.
- Natural predation by sharks and orcas can drive turtles into shallow water, causing stranding.
- Natural disease and weakness.

## Conservation solutions

<Note>
  The leatherback is listed on **CITES Appendix I**, prohibiting all international commercial trade in the species, its eggs, and its parts.
</Note>

Thailand's response to leatherback turtle threats combines legal protection with active rescue and rehabilitation protocols.

**Legal protection**
The species is fully protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535 (1992) and its 2019 amendment. Hunting, trading, possessing, or breeding leatherbacks — including eggs and remains — carries strict criminal penalties.

**Rescue when a turtle is caught in fishing gear**
- If caught in a net: cut away the net fragments, check for wounds, and release immediately if the turtle is healthy.
- If a hook is visible: do not pull the line forcefully. Push the barbed end through until it exits, cut the barb with pliers, then withdraw the hook. Never attempt to remove a deeply swallowed hook — report it immediately so a veterinarian can perform surgery.

**First aid for a stranded or injured turtle**
- Move the turtle to a shaded, quiet location.
- Cover the shell with a damp cloth to maintain moisture; keep the nostrils clear.
- If the turtle appears sick, lethargic, or badly wounded, transport it on an appropriate stretcher or tray — without submerging it — to a rehabilitation facility within 4 hours.
- If water aspiration is suspected, tilt the turtle with its rear end raised higher than its head to drain fluid from the lungs.
- In rehabilitation, house the turtle in a smooth-surfaced pool deep enough to float but not so deep that it cannot surface to breathe easily. Provide food and medication under veterinary supervision until the turtle is strong enough for release.

**Goal**
All these measures aim to reduce mortality caused by human activity and to maintain a viable leatherback population in Thai waters.
